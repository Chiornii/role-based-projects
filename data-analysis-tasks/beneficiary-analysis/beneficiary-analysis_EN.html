Next, we will examine the total benefit costs by country and the average per employee.

Due to the large number of employees, the overall benefit costs in India are higher. However, on average, one employee in Turkey receives 13.49 times more in benefits than one in India. This may indicate either higher-level roles in Turkey or a substantial imbalance in benefit distribution between the countries. Lets now compare identical roles across both countries. Turkey has 11 roles, 7 of which also exist in India.

The comparison shows a noticeable shift toward higher benefit spending in Turkey, except for one role Country Manager. In both countries, there is one person in this role, but in India, it is an expatriate, which explains the higher cost. This situation highlights the lack of a unified compensation policy across countries and roles.

To draw final conclusions, lets look at benefit spending by role. The dataset includes 75 roles across the two countries. For easier visualization, lets focus on the top 10 roles, which account for 71.63% of the total costs.

Well now examine the average benefit amounts per role across both countries to understand how payouts are distributed among positions.

Out of the 75 roles, the top 8 (~11% of all roles) by average benefit level account for about 60% of the total benefit amount, based on the average values per role. Four employees receiving abnormally high benefits fall within these eight roles, likely having a significant impact on the overall share.

Executive Summary

1. Establish fair compensation boundaries by role, not by country

The charts show that the company pays different benefit amounts for the same role depending on the country. Instead of focusing solely on location, it's better to set global compensation frameworks for each role, adjusting for economic conditions using purchasing power indices such as the Purchasing Power Index (Numbeo) or World Bank PLI.

This is important for:

Fairness among employees
Motivation and retention
Transparency and predictability in HR policy

With the rise of remote work and global hiring, relying solely on "local rates" is becoming outdated.

2. Verify the justification for the concentration of benefits in a limited number of roles

Out of 75 roles, the top 8 (~11%) by average benefit level account for around 60% of the total benefit amount, based on average role values. This may reflect the companys hierarchical structure. In addition, four employees with abnormally high benefits significantly influence the statistics, but such concentration in the top 8 roles requires further scrutiny regarding:

Alignment of role and cost level
Fairness and market competitiveness of compensation

A further audit is recommended: analyze the business value of these roles and compare their compensation levels to market benchmarks using public sources such as Glassdoor, PayScale, or Mercer reports.

3. Assess the impact of benefits on employee retention

It is recommended to analyze how benefits affect employee turnover. Comparing benefit levels with retention trends can help assess the effectiveness of the benefits package as a retention tool.

4. Gradual alignment of compensation policy

A strategy should be developed to gradually align approaches to benefits:

In countries with low payouts gradual increases
In countries with excessive costs optimization without losing appeal

It is recommended to start with the top 10 roles by benefit volume, as this is where:

  1. the biggest imbalances are likely (underpayment or overpayment),
  2. the bulk of the costs lies.
